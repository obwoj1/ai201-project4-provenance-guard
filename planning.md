## Problem & Architecture Narrative

Creative platforms need a way to tell audiences whether a piece of text was
written by a human or generated by AI — not to police creativity, but to protect
attribution and give readers honest context. Provenance Guard is a backend any
such platform could plug into.

**The path a single submission takes:** A creator submits text to `POST /submit`
with their `creator_id`. The text is sent through **two independent detection
signals** — a Groq LLM judgment (semantic) and stylometric heuristics
(structural). Each returns a 0.0–1.0 AI-likeness score. Those two scores are
combined by a weighted scorer into a single **confidence** value, which is mapped
to one of **three transparency labels**. The result is assigned a UUID
`content_id`, written to the **audit log**, and returned to the creator. If a
creator disputes the result, `POST /appeal` looks up that `content_id`, records
their reasoning, flips the status to `under_review`, and re-writes the log.

**Narrative.** The submission flow fans the raw text out to two independent
signals, merges their scores into one weighted confidence, maps that to a label,
logs everything, and returns it. The appeal flow is a focused mutation: find the
prior decision by `content_id`, attach the creator's reasoning, mark it
`under_review`, and persist — leaving a complete paper trail of both the original
decision and the contest.

## 1. Detection Signals

The system uses **two genuinely distinct signals** — one semantic, one
structural — so the combination is more informative than either alone.

### Signal A — Groq LLM (semantic coherence)
- **Measures:** holistically whether the text *reads* as human or AI, using the
  model's broad exposure to both. Captures tone, phrasing patterns, and the
  "too-polished, hedge-everything" register typical of AI prose.
- **Output:** a float 0.0 (human) → 1.0 (AI), parsed from a forced-JSON response
  `{"score", "reasoning"}`. On any parse/network failure it returns **0.5**
  (neutral) so a bad call never crashes a request or biases the verdict.
- **Blind spot:** it can be fooled by lightly-edited AI text and may over-flag
  formal-but-human writing (academics, non-native speakers). It is also
  non-deterministic and opaque — it can't *prove* why it scored something.

### Signal B — Stylometric heuristics (structural statistics)
Pure Python, no libraries. Three sub-metrics, each normalized to 0–1, then
averaged (40% sentence uniformity, 40% type-token, 20% punctuation):
- **Sentence-length uniformity** — AI keeps sentences similar in length (low
  coefficient of variation); humans vary. Lower variation → more AI-like.
- **Type-token ratio** — unique words ÷ total words. AI reuses vocabulary;
  lower diversity → more AI-like.
- **Punctuation density** — punctuation chars ÷ total chars, benchmarked against
  a ~3% human reference; very sparse punctuation → slightly more AI-like.
- **Output:** a float 0.0–1.0.
- **Blind spot:** it's purely surface-level. A short human poem with deliberate
  repetition and simple vocabulary can look "uniform" and score AI-like; a long,
  varied AI essay can look human. It has no understanding of *meaning*.

**Why this pairing:** Signal A understands meaning but is opaque and gameable;
Signal B is transparent and deterministic but shallow. Their errors are
uncorrelated, so blending them dampens each one's individual blind spot.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

- **Combination:** `confidence = 0.6 * groq_score + 0.4 * stylo_score`, rounded
  to 2 decimals. The LLM gets the larger weight because it's the stronger judge;
  the stylometric signal is a cheap, deterministic cross-check that pulls
  confident-but-wrong LLM calls back toward the middle.
- **What 0.6 means to the system:** *leaning AI, but not confidently* — it lands
  in the **Uncertain** band on purpose. We never force a binary at 0.5. A score
  is a position on a spectrum, and the middle band exists precisely so the system
  can say "we're not sure" instead of guessing.
- **Thresholds (chosen to favor creators — see false-positive note):**

  | Confidence | Attribution | Meaning |
  |---|---|---|
  | **≥ 0.70** | `likely_ai` | both signals must broadly agree it's AI |
  | **0.40 – 0.69** | `uncertain` | mixed / weak signal |
  | **< 0.40** | `likely_human` | the default benefit of the doubt |

- **False-positive asymmetry:** on a writing platform, wrongly branding a human's
  work as AI is the more harmful error. The bar for `likely_ai` is deliberately
  high (0.70) and the `uncertain` band is wide, so borderline work is labeled
  "uncertain" rather than accused.
- **How tested:** four reference inputs (clearly-AI, clearly-human, formal-human,
  lightly-edited-AI) are run and the combined + individual scores inspected to
  confirm meaningful spread and correct banding (see README).

## 3. Transparency Label Design

Three plain-language variants a non-technical reader sees. Exact text:

| Confidence | Label text (verbatim) |
|---|---|
| ≥ 0.70 | **⚠️ Likely AI-Generated** |
| 0.40 – 0.69 | **🔍 Uncertain Origin** |
| < 0.40 | **✅ Likely Human-Written** |

The icon + short phrase is readable at a glance; the band names ("uncertain")
communicate confidence without exposing a raw number that a lay reader would
misinterpret. The middle band intentionally avoids any accusation.

## 4. Appeals Workflow

- **Who can appeal:** the creator of a classified submission (in a real
  deployment, authenticated as the `creator_id` on the entry).
- **What they provide:** the `content_id` of the decision and free-text
  `creator_reasoning` explaining why they believe it's wrong.
- **What the system does:** looks up the entry by `content_id` (404 if unknown),
  sets `status` → `under_review`, stores the reasoning in `appeal_reasoning`,
  rewrites the log, and returns a confirmation. No automated re-classification —
  appeals route to humans.
- **What a reviewer sees:** the original decision (both signal scores, combined
  confidence, label, timestamp) *and* the creator's reasoning, side by side in
  the same audit entry — everything needed to adjudicate, queryable via
  `GET /log` (filter on `status == "under_review"`).

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

1. **Repetitive, simple-vocabulary poetry.** A human poem built on refrains and
   plain words ("I rise, I rise, I rise again") has low sentence-length variance
   and a low type-token ratio — exactly the structural fingerprint the
   stylometric signal reads as AI. The LLM signal usually rescues it, but a
   heavily repetitive piece can be wrongly nudged toward `likely_ai`.
2. **Formal non-native-English prose.** Careful, formal writing from a non-native
   speaker shares surface features with AI (even sentence rhythm, hedged tone),
   so both signals can drift up together. This is the most dangerous false
   positive — and the exact scenario the appeals workflow and high AI-threshold
   exist to protect.
3. **Very short inputs (one line).** With too few sentences/words, stylometric
   metrics are statistically meaningless; the signal returns ~0.5 (neutral) by
   design rather than pretending to a verdict.

## AI Tool Plan

How AI tooling was directed across the three implementation milestones, with the
spec sections fed in and the verification step for each.

- **M3 — submission endpoint + first signal.**
  *Provide:* §1 Detection Signals (Signal A) + the Architecture diagram.
  *Ask for:* the Flask app skeleton with a `POST /submit` route and the
  `groq_signal(text)` function returning a 0–1 float with a 0.5 fallback.
  *Verify:* call `groq_signal` directly on clearly-AI and clearly-human samples
  and confirm the scores and JSON parsing before wiring it into the route; add
  the structured audit log and `GET /log`.

- **M4 — second signal + confidence scoring.**
  *Provide:* §1 Detection Signals (Signal B) + §2 Uncertainty Representation +
  diagram.
  *Ask for:* `stylometric_signal(text)` and `combine_scores` / `get_label`.
  *Verify:* run the 4 reference inputs and confirm the combined score spreads
  across all three bands and matches the specified thresholds exactly (AI tools
  sometimes implement thresholds that silently diverge — check the boundaries).

- **M5 — production layer.**
  *Provide:* §3 Label Variants + §4 Appeals Workflow + diagram.
  *Ask for:* the label-mapping function and the `POST /appeal` endpoint, plus
  Flask-Limiter config.
  *Verify:* confirm all three label variants are reachable, that an appeal flips
  `status` to `under_review` and populates `appeal_reasoning`, and that the
  rate-limit returns 429 after the configured threshold.

## Design Decisions (summary)

- **Two weighted signals (60/40)** — strong-but-opaque LLM + transparent
  structural cross-check.
- **Creator-favoring thresholds** — high bar for `likely_ai`, wide `uncertain`
  band, to minimize the costly false positive.
- **Safe neutral defaults** — parse failures and too-short inputs return 0.5.
- **JSON audit log** — human-readable and simple for this scope; SQLite/Postgres
  in production.
- **In-memory rate limiting** — fine for a single-process demo; Redis in
  production.
